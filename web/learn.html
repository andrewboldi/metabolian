What is a metabolic pathway?

Metabolism is the chemistry of being alive — thousands of small reactions that build, break down, and rearrange molecules. A pathway is a connected sequence of those reactions. Here's how to read one.

Reactions, chained together

A single reaction turns one set of molecules (substrates) into another (products), almost always sped up by a protein catalyst called an enzyme. String reactions together — the product of one becoming the substrate of the next — and you have a pathway. Glycolysis, for instance, is ten reactions that convert glucose into pyruvate.

Anabolism vs. catabolism

  • Catabolism breaks large molecules down to release energy — glucose to pyruvate, fats to acetyl-CoA.
  • Anabolism builds complex molecules up, spending energy — making fatty acids, nucleotides, and proteins.

The cell's energy currency, ATP, and its electron carriers, NAD⁺/NADH and FAD/FADH₂, couple these two directions together.

Enzymes and EC numbers

Every enzyme has a four-part EC number classifying the reaction it catalyzes — 2.7.1.1 is hexokinase, a transferase that moves a phosphate onto glucose. In Metabolian, EC numbers, gene symbols, and UniProt accessions appear as monospace chips on every enzyme, and each one opens its 3D structure.

Cofactors and coenzymes

Many enzymes need a non-protein helper: a metal ion like Mg²⁺, or a coenzyme like NAD⁺ derived from a vitamin. These are drawn as cofactor arrows feeding into a reaction.

Regulation — how flux is controlled

Pathways aren't always on. Cells tune them through several mechanisms, and Metabolian gives each its own arrow:

  • Activation and inhibition — a molecule speeds up or slows an enzyme.
  • Allosteric control — an effector binds away from the active site and changes the enzyme's shape.
  • Feedback inhibition — a pathway's end product shuts off its own first committed step.
  • Covalent modification — phosphorylation and other tags flip enzymes on or off.
  • Transcriptional control — the cell makes more or less of the enzyme itself.

See all of them in the grammar of metabolism.
