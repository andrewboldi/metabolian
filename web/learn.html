How to read a metabolic pathway

Metabolism is the chemistry of being alive — thousands of small reactions that build, break down, and rearrange molecules. A pathway is a connected sequence of them. This page teaches the notation, then hands you the sheet.

01Reactions, chained together

A single reaction turns one set of molecules — the substrates — into another, the products, almost always sped up by a protein catalyst called an enzyme. String reactions together so the product of one becomes the substrate of the next, and you have a pathway. Glycolysis, for instance, is ten reactions that convert glucose into pyruvate.

That chaining is the whole reason a wall chart works. Any one reaction is a fact; a pathway is an argument about where matter and energy go.

02Anabolism and catabolism

  • Catabolism breaks large molecules down and releases energy — glucose to pyruvate, fats to acetyl-CoA.
  • Anabolism builds complex molecules up and spends energy — making fatty acids, nucleotides, and proteins.

The cell's energy currency, ATP, and its electron carriers, NAD⁺/NADH and FAD/FADH₂, couple the two directions together. This is why the chart is worth reading as a whole rather than one pathway at a time: the carriers are the wiring between otherwise unrelated regions of the sheet.

03Enzymes and EC numbers

Every enzyme has a four-part EC number classifying the reaction it catalyses. 2.7.1.1 is hexokinase: class 2 a transferase, 2.7 transferring a phosphorus group, 2.7.1 onto an alcohol, and the final digit naming the specific activity. Read left to right, an EC number is a sentence about chemistry.

In Metabolian, EC numbers appear as monospace chips on essentially every enzyme — 12,133 of 12,182 enzyme records carry one. Gene symbols and UniProt accessions are rarer: about 383 records have them today, and those are the enzymes that open directly in the 3D structure viewer. If an enzyme has no structure link, that is a gap in our annotation, not a claim that no structure exists.

04Cofactors and coenzymes

Many enzymes need a non-protein helper: a metal ion like Mg²⁺, or a coenzyme like NAD⁺ derived from a vitamin. On the sheet these do not sit in the main line of flow — they arrive on their own dashed arc, curving into the reaction and away again. That convention is Michal's, and it is what keeps a dense sheet readable: the backbone of a pathway stays a straight line, and everything that merely assists bends around it.

05Regulation — how flux is controlled

Pathways are not always on. Cells tune them through several distinct mechanisms, and each gets its own arrow, drawn below at the size and stroke the chart actually uses. The shape carries the meaning: a bar always stops something, a solid triangle always speeds it up, and a dashed line always means the effector acts somewhere other than the active site.

Regulatory arrows
SpecimenTypeWhat it means
Activation A molecule binds an enzyme and speeds it up.
Inhibition A molecule binds an enzyme and slows it down.
Allosteric inhibition The effector binds away from the active site and changes the enzyme's shape. Dashed, because it never touches the chemistry directly.
Feedback inhibition A pathway's end product shuts off its own first committed step — the cell's thermostat.
Covalent modification Phosphorylation and other tags are attached to the enzyme, flipping it on or off. The diamond marks a chemical change to the catalyst itself.
Transcriptional activation The cell makes more of the enzyme. Slower than the others by hours, not milliseconds.
Transcriptional repression The cell makes less of the enzyme.

All 30 arrow types live in the grammar of metabolism. Regulation is also the thinnest part of the atlas today — 224 regulatory links against 26,510 reactions — so a pathway drawn without regulators is usually a statement about our coverage, not about the biology. The known limits are stated in full.

06Reading the sheet

Open the chart. It is drawn in Michal's visual language, so a handful of marks carry the whole notation:

Marks on the sheet
SpecimenMarkMeans
Boxed name A metabolite, with its condensed formula set alongside in monospace.
Name on the arrow The enzyme catalysing that step, with its EC number beneath. Click it to open the structure.
Curved arc A cofactor or coenzyme couple joining the reaction and leaving again — ATP/ADP, NAD⁺/NADH.
Framed region A named territory of the sheet — a compartment or a sub-pathway, titled at its corner.

Mass flows along the solid arrows. Everything coloured is a claim about control rather than matter:

Mass-flow arrows
SpecimenTypeMeans
Substrate Metabolite consumed by a reaction.
Product Metabolite produced by a reaction.
Reversible Runs both ways under physiological conditions. Which way it actually runs depends on concentrations.
Cofactor A helper required for catalysis, not consumed as a substrate.
Catalyses Links an enzyme to the reaction it accelerates. The open circle means it is not consumed.

Search glucose in Explore and follow it step by step to pyruvate. Ten reactions later you will have walked the most famous pathway in biochemistry, and every arrow you passed will have a source you can open.

07Go deeper

Metabolian is an index into these, not a replacement for them. Where an entry here carries a high confidence, it usually means one of the first two agreed with the chemistry:

  • Rhea — expert-annotated, mass-balanced reaction chemistry.
  • MetaNetX — reconciles reaction and metabolite identifiers across databases.
  • KEGG PATHWAY — the reference pathway maps.
  • Reactome — curated human pathways with rich annotation.
  • AlphaFold DB — predicted structures for nearly every protein.

How those sources are weighted, and where the atlas is still thin, is set out in method and sources.
